before I moved to los angeles,

my mother took me shopping

for blazers, silk dresses printed

with flowers, sandals with

too-steep wedge heels.

we collected shades of turquoise,

terra cotta, peach—a palette

we associated with the southwest.

we were buying things to match

my future home, as we imagined it;

to suit the warmer climate;

to clothe me for the job I didn’t have yet

but imagined I would soon.

my mom insisted i try on

a denim jacket, pale pink. i knew i’d

never wear it but forgot that when

the sales associate at macy’s

said i looked “so L.A.” in it.

the blazers led a lonely life

inside my closet; the high heels

gathered dust. i needed flat shoes

that would carry me four miles

every morning to my internship.

my feet never hurt—at first.

i spent my walks rehearsing

how i would recount them

someday

when i “made it.” i thought,

someday this will be the prologue

to my cinderella story.

gradually, though, my feet

began to lose their willingness

to bear my weight. the sun dimmed.

and yes, my soles were sore

the day i packed my dreams inside a car

and drove back east.
